Every day across Australia, road crashes send over a hundred people to hospital. While fatalities draw the attention of media and policymakers, non-fatal hospitalisations represent a far greater and less visible burden — on individuals, families, and the healthcare system. This data story examines eleven years of hospitalised road crash injuries, revealing who bears the highest risk, how that burden is shifting, and where disparities remain most acute.

01

A Decade of Rising Hospitalisations

National hospitalised injuries climbed steadily from 33,487 in 2011 to a peak of 38,875 in 2021 — a 16 % increase over ten years. A brief dip occurred in 2020 as COVID-19 travel restrictions curtailed road exposure, before fully recovering the following year. The regulation changes in 2012 and 2017 could also have affected to the numbers, but the overall trend is clear: hospitalisations have been on the rise, underscoring the need for renewed focus on road safety measures and injury prevention strategies.

02

Who Gets Hurt on Our Roads?

Car occupants consistently account for roughly half of all hospitalisations. Yet the most striking trend is the rise of pedal cyclists, whose numbers grew from 5,314 in 2011 to 8,039 in 2021 — a 51 % increase — tracking Australia's cycling boom. Motorcyclists remain the second-largest group at around 22 % each year. Select a year to compare how the landscape has changed.

03

Age & Gender: A Persistent Pattern

Working-age adults aged 40–64 bear the greatest absolute hospitalisation burden. Across every age group and every year, males account for approximately two-thirds of hospitalisations. The male–female gap is starkest among young adults (17–25), consistent with international evidence that young men engage in higher-risk driving behaviour.

04

Urban, Regional, Remote: Where Crashes Happen

Major cities account for the majority of absolute hospitalisations due to population concentration. However, remote Australia faces a disproportionate per-capita risk — higher speeds, longer distances from trauma care, and limited road infrastructure all compound severity. Regional areas show a steady and concerning share across all eleven years.

State by State: Raw Numbers & Context

New South Wales and Victoria dominate in absolute terms, reflecting their larger populations. Direct comparisons without per-capita normalisation can be misleading — the Northern Territory's figures represent a significantly higher rate relative to its population size. This visualisation presents absolute counts only; per-capita analysis is beyond the current dataset scope.

06

A Hidden Disparity: First Nations Communities

First Nations Australians face a disproportionate road crash injury burden, particularly in remote areas. While raw counts are smaller than for non-Indigenous Australians — partly reflecting population size — First Nations hospitalisations grew by over 50 % from 2011 to 2021. The concentration of injuries in remote regions, where emergency services are least accessible, amplifies the severity and consequence of these events.
